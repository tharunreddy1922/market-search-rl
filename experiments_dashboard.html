Key Findings Across All Experiments

DQN and Double-DQN consistently rank 1st–2nd in reward across all four experiments, with their emergent price-discount behaviour (−16% to −21%) persisting under every environmental change tested.
PPO achieves competitive completion rates (97–100%) but consistently scores lower reward because it does not learn the price-seeking routing strategy that the off-policy DQN family discovers. At equal episode budgets, on-policy methods are less sample-efficient for this task.
Exp 2 (10 goods/store) is the hardest experiment: all agents' completion rates fall, Greedy overtakes DQN in reward for the first time, and PPO performs worst of all. Specialised store coverage penalises agents that do not explicitly plan multi-store routing.
Exp 3 (80% availability) is the easiest: Q-Learning nearly closes the gap on DQN (+475 vs +567), showing that neural generalisation matters most when the environment is uncertain.
TimeAware Heuristic consistently achieves the highest brand preference score (4.48–4.57/5) across all experiments — no learned agent matches its quality-selection behaviour.

Exp 0 Analysis: PPO vs Off-Policy Methods

PPO achieves strong completion (97.9%) but only +217 reward — comparable to the TimeAware Heuristic. At 1,000 training episodes, PPO's on-policy nature means it has seen far fewer unique state transitions than DQN's experience replay buffer allows.
The critical gap is price optimisation: DQN achieves −18.6% discount, PPO only −5.0%. PPO's policy gradient updates push toward high-completion policies first; the subtler discount-routing signal requires more experience to learn.
DQN marginally outperforms Double-DQN at this episode budget, suggesting the overestimation correction of Double-DQN matters more at larger training scales (as seen in the 10,000-episode dissertation results where Double-DQN wins).

Exp 1 Analysis: Impact of Extended Time + Revisiting

DQN benefits most from extra time (+555 vs +473 baseline, +17%), exploiting the revisiting mechanic to circle back to low-premium stores and squeezing out −20.4% average discount — the best price performance across all experiments.
Double-DQN reward actually drops (+444 vs +485 baseline). With more time available, the agent explores more aggressively, visiting 2.77 stores on average vs 2.31 at baseline. The longer episode increases variance in reward, and the additional store-visit penalties outweigh the gains from revisiting.
Q-Learning struggles with the longer horizon — its coarser state hash (20-min time buckets) is less adapted to the 180-min window, reducing generalisation. Completion drops to 88.5% despite more available time.
PPO's reward falls most severely (+118 vs +217 baseline). The on-policy agent cannot exploit the revisit mechanic efficiently at this training budget because it rarely discovers revisiting in its limited experience.

Exp 2 Analysis: Impact of Restricted Store Coverage

Exp 2 is the most disruptive experiment. Completion rates drop for every agent except Greedy (which remains at 100%). The specialisation constraint removes the random coverage benefit that made the baseline solvable with a simple greedy strategy.
Greedy overtakes DQN in reward (+318 vs +314) for the first time. Its coverage-maximising routing rule — visiting the store with the best aggregate brand score for remaining goods — exactly matches the specialisation structure, making it the most effective strategy for guaranteed coverage.
Double-DQN remains the best RL agent (+360) because its neural network can generalise across store-good patterns, but even it only achieves 86.8% completion — revealing the limits of the current training budget on a harder routing problem.
PPO collapses to −101 reward and 63.4% completion. Without the reliable coverage structure of the baseline, PPO's on-policy exploration rarely encounters the specific multi-store routes needed to complete the list, making the sparse terminal reward very hard to learn from.
Quality-Threshold Heuristic falls to +23 reward (76.2% completion) — its dual threshold filters out too many purchase opportunities in the restricted environment, stranding goods that only appear at stores with slightly below-threshold brands.

Exp 3 Analysis: Impact of Higher Availability (80%)

All agents improve in both reward and completion. The most notable shift is Q-Learning, which rises from +395 to +475 — nearly closing the gap on DQN (+567). With less availability uncertainty, the tabular agent's coarser state representation is sufficient, and it generalises well.
DQN achieves its best price discount across all experiments (−20.8%), showing that higher availability gives the agent more purchase opportunities to be selective, consistently picking the lowest-premium brand available for each good.
PPO more than doubles its reward (+277 vs +217 baseline) and achieves near-perfect completion (99.9%). Higher availability means PPO's simpler policy quickly learns "buy anything available" which suffices for completion — confirming that PPO's weakness at baseline is the stockout-navigation challenge, not policy optimisation per se.
The Quality-Threshold Heuristic improves dramatically (completion 98.5% vs 94.9% baseline) — with 80% availability, its strict dual-threshold filters are satisfied far more often, so it rarely fails to find a qualifying item.
TimeAware Heuristic achieves the highest preference score across all experiments (4.57/5) at 80% availability — the combination of adaptive quality thresholds and abundant preferred brands produces near-optimal quality purchasing.
